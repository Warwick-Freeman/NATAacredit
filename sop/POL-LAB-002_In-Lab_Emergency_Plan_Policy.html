1. Purpose

Patients monitored in the sleep lab, especially those with sleep apnea or pulmonary issues, face potential cardiopulmonary risks. The technologist must swiftly detect and respond to these events, while protocols address other emergencies. Adherence to medical and environmental procedures ensures safety for patients and staff.

2. Policy

All personnel who interact with patients must be familiar with all medical and environmental emergency procedures and the location of emergency equipment (e.g., barrier mask for resuscitation, AED). All sleep personnel responsible for patient care will maintain a current certificate in basic life support, including cognitive and skills training (in-person active training (e.g., in-person practice on a manikin). All sleep personnel responsible for patient care will be able to recognize situations that would activate an emergency response.

These emergency procedures will be followed in all medical and environmental emergencies. Sleep staff will err on the side of caution if in doubt, considering the medical and cardiopulmonary history of the patient. Any questions, concerns or problems regarding emergency procedures will be addressed immediately to Dr. __ (insert name) ____, the director who can be paged by calling (XXX) XXX-XXXX (insert direct phone contact). Dr. __ (insert name) ____, will review and modify the medical emergency procedures annually.

Types of emergencies

  • cardiopulmonary
  • neurological (seizure and strokes)
  • psychiatric (suicidal ideation)
  • belligerent patient
  • environmental (fire, severe weather, bomb threat)

3. Procedure

The following procedure will be followed as applicable to the number of technologists available.

General emergency procedure

One technologist on duty

Call emergency personnel on 911.

Provide the following information: 1.2.1 name and physical address of the sleep lab

phone number

nature of emergency

detailed instructions for remote access to the lab ___ Nexus 360___ (i.e., Knox Box, door access code)

Obtain AED, which is located at___Nexus 360 ______.

Assess the patient, turn on AED and follow the prompts, check rhythm/shock if indicated (repeat every 2 minutes) initiate CPR following the basic life support (BLS) guidelines (if applicable) and ensure patient safety.

Stay with the patient following emergency procedures until ER personnel arrive.

Contact the director and other pertinent lab personnel.

Once the patient has been removed from the sleep lab or ER has been resolved, complete appropriate documentation in the medical record.

Complete an unusual occurrence report documenting the event, procedures followed, actions taken, outcome and recommendations for improvement, if warranted.

Two technologists on duty

Technologist One:

Call for help and send a second technologist to call 911 and obtain AED.

Assess the patient, initiate CPR till AED arrives. When the AED arrives, turn on AED and follow the prompts; check rhythm/shock if indicated (repeat every 2 minutes); resume CPR following the BLS guidelines (if applicable) and ensure patient safety.

Technologist Two:

Call emergency personnel on 911.

Provide the following information:

name and physical address of the sleep lab

phone number

nature of the emergency

detailed instructions for entry access to the lab

Obtain AED, which is located___Nexus 360 ________.

Monitor other patients in the sleep lab.

Once ER personnel arrive, open the door and direct personnel to the emergency.

Continue to monitor other patients; complete the ongoing sleep services.

Once the emergency has been resolved, assist Technologist One in completing the unusual occurrence report documenting the event including procedures followed, actions taken, outcome and recommendations for improvement, if warranted.

Restock the emergency cart: e.g., medications or equipment used or oxygen supplies.

Return AED to proper location, if used.

Cardiopulmonary

Enter patient room and try to arouse the patient if you identify any of the following:

asystole greater than 10 seconds (check backup lead first)

ventricular tachycardia greater than 10 seconds

ventricular fibrillation

apnea greater than 2 minutes

A new onset of:

greater than 6 PVCs per minute for 2 minutes or more

more than 2 runs of 6 or more PVCs

persistent bigeminy or trigeminy

atrial flutter or fibrillation

clinically symptomatic tachycardia or bradycardia

any arrhythmia, EEG phenomenon, respiratory event, or patient-reported symptom that, in the opinion of the sleep technologist, may lead to an emergency situation.

If the patient remains unresponsive, initiate CPR and call emergency services (911) immediately.

Continue CPR following the general emergency procedures (activate AED if warranted) until ER personnel arrives.

Neurological (seizure)

Assess the patient; if the patient is experiencing a seizure, position the patient on their side and protect the patient from injury.

Maintain a patent airway and monitor oxygen saturation levels, providing oxygen if necessary.

Monitor and keep track of EKG, rate, and rhythm and continue study.

Continue video and PSG recordings.

If the patient has no history of seizure, contact emergency personnel (911).

Observe and record:

time and length of seizure

type of movements (unilateral or bilateral)

eye movements

consciousness

Continue monitoring until ER personnel arrive.

Contact the lab director or on-call physician for further directions.

Document all data (including EEG data in the patient record).

Stroke

Follow FAST protocol to assess the patient for signs of possible stroke:

F---Facial droop (have patient show teeth or smile)

A---Arm drift (patient closes eyes and extends both arms straight out, with palms up for 10 seconds)

S---Abnormal speech

T---Time to call 911 (EMS)

Other Symptoms

sudden numbness or weakness in face, arms or legs (especially one side of the body)

sudden confusion

sudden severe headache

Call 911 immediately (activate EMS system). Notify EMS responders of possible stroke.

Assess the patient using a stroke screening tool and give oxygen as needed.

Protect patient and support ABCs (airway, breathing, and circulation).

Transport the patient as quickly as possible.

Assess neurological status while the patient is being transported. Provide patient status/paperwork to EMS.

Psychiatric (suicidal ideation)

  1. Assess the situation and determine if staff or patients are in immediate danger.
  2. Activate emergency personnel; call 911 immediately.
  3. Contact the lab director or on-call physician if patient has expressed threats of suicide or suicidal ideation.
  4. Remain calm, listen attentively to the patient, and do not offer advice.
  5. Always keep the patient within view.
  6. Once ER personnel transport the patient, provide copies of all protected health information (PHI) to go with the patient, if possible.
  7. Document all information related to the situation in the patient’s medical record.

Environmental Emergency

In the event of an environmental emergency, sleep lab personnel are to follow the procedures as appropriate to the emergency. Ensure a battery-operated flashlight and radio are available and in working order at all times.

The emergencies are defined as follows:

fire

severe weather conditions (tornado, flood, etc.)

bomb threat

belligerent patient

other

Fire

In the event of a fire, sleep personnel shall follow appropriate procedures for evacuation of the patients in the lab if needed.

Implement R A C E protocol:

Rescue any and all patients or visitors in immediate danger.

Alarm, pull the nearest alarm box.

Call 911 and give the location of the fire.

Evacuate to a safe predesignated area.

When the fire is discovered:

Immediately contact emergency personnel; dial 911.

Provide location of the fire

name of lab

location or room of the fire

physical address of lab

phone number of the lab

contact person’s name and title

Provide the number of individuals in the lab at the time of the fire.

Provide location of where the patients have been moved.

Move patients to the designated area as identified in the evacuation route plan posted in each testing room.

Close the doors to the room where the fire is located.

Once all patients and staff have congregated at the designated location, determine if all staff and patients are present.

Close all lab doors and wait for emergency personnel.

Do not attempt to go back into the building until it has been cleared to do so by the fire department.

  1. Severe weather conditions

In the event of a severe weather alert or watch, such as tornado, flood, hurricane, etc., the staff shall protect the health, safety and well-being of all patients.

In case of a severe weather emergency in the area, advance notice is usually announced by local authorities.

Do not release patients unless it is safe to do so.

In the event of a tornado, all sleep personnel shall move the patients to the basement or designated safe location in the lab away from windows.

All sleep personnel shall provide clinical and medical assistance needed to accommodate patients as appropriate.

All sleep personnel should secure the lab and move essential records and equipment to higher ground if possible. Turn off all electrical switches and disconnect all electrical equipment. Do not touch electrical equipment if standing in water.

If in an area with windows, find protection under a table or other furniture out of the way of flying glass.

Keep in touch with local police or fire department to determine the status of the weather emergency.

Once the emergency has ceased, provide any needed assistance to patients. Determine if it is safe to continue the services; if not, terminate, reschedule and discharge the patient.

Contact the lab director and any other pertinent lab staff.

Complete an unusual occurrence report documenting the event, procedures followed, actions taken and outcome. The report should include any recommendations for future modifications to the emergency plan if needed.

  1. Bomb threat

All bomb threats should be treated as real.

If a threatening call is received, attempt to obtain as much information as possible (who, where, what and when). If possible, use the bomb threat checklist stored at each phone.

Stay calm.

Keep the caller on the phone as long as possible; DO NOT HANG UP even if the caller disconnects the call.

While on the phone, attempt to notify another staff member of what is occurring (e.g., write a note, make a gesture) in order to contact the authorities; call 911.

Pay attention to the time of the call and any details provided from the caller:

Where is the bomb?

When will it go off?

Type of bomb?

Pay attention to any identifiable sounds on the phone (e.g., background noise).

If the caller terminates the call, contact emergency personnel (911) from a different phone and await instructions.

Contact director and other pertinent sleep lab personnel.

Move and evacuate patients to a safe location if the location of the bomb is within the lab.

Once the bomb threat is alleviated, complete an unusual occurrence report detailing the event, procedures followed, actions taken, and outcome. The report should include recommendations for improving the plan, if warranted

  1. Belligerent patient

If there is a situation in which the patient makes the staff uncomfortable, report the behavior to the director or manager immediately.

Assess the severity of the situation and determine immediate response. If the patient’s agitation is potentially dangerous to other patients or staff, contact 911 immediately and follow directions.

Do not attempt to confront the patient; stay calm and observe the patient while keeping safely out of harm’s way.
