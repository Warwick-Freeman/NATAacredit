1. Purpose

Provide adequate information to perform quality polysomnograms on the neonate and infant. Neonate generally refers to a child up to 1 month of age after birth, and infant generally refers to a child between 1 month and 12 months of age. If the infant is premature (i.e., less than 38 weeks gestation), the age is adjusted from the gestational age.

2. Indications

Sleep induces changes in the function and control of the respiratory system. These changes may result in clinically significant abnormalities in upper-airway function and gas exchange in normal children and those with underlying respiratory or central nervous system disease.

Neonates and infants, especially those born prematurely, frequently experience apnea or bradycardia during sleep and feeding in the first several weeks of life. Virtually all neonates and infants under 1,000 g will experience apnea. Apnea may be mixed, central, or obstructive. Periodic breathing or respiratory dysrhythmia also may be present.

A brief resolved unexplained event (BRUE), is an episode of apnea, color change (e.g., pallor, cyanosis or erythema), hypotonia, or unresponsiveness that the observer believes to be life threatening to the infant and for which some intervention (e.g., stimulation, shaking, and/or cardiopulmonary resuscitation) is felt to be required. The major concerns following one of these episodes relate to the specific etiology as well as the risk of recurrent events and death.

Although polysomnography may not be indicated for routine evaluation of neonates and infants with an uncomplicated apparent life-threatening event (ALTE), it may be helpful in defining the frequency and type of apnea and the extent of cardiac, blood gas, and sleep alterations in infants with apnea or ALTE. These patients include infants with suspected obstructive sleep apnea, those with recurrent isolated bradycardia without central apnea, and those suspected to have abnormal respiratory control.

Other reasons include, but are not limited to, chronic lung disease, neuromuscular disease, gastroesophageal reflux with or without apnea, craniofacial malformations and sleep-related seizures.

3. Policy

If a lab does not have the expertise to perform a neonatal or infant polysomnogram, the patient should be sent to an accredited sleep disorders facility that has this capability.

Neonates will be scheduled for daytime or nighttime testing in the NICU, nursery or in the sleep disorders facility with a NICU nurse available, if needed. Infants usually will be studied in the sleep lab with adequate medical personnel coverage.

Because it is the policy of the NICU that neonates will be disturbed as little as possible, application of electrodes, in some circumstances, may need to be applied in conjunction with nursing procedures during the “hands-on” periods. “hands-on” time is usually every 3 to 4 hours and no longer than 10 to 15 minutes. Organizing the electrode and sensor application is essential.

Patient-to-technologist ratio will be 1:1.

Feedings depend on the nature of the neonate/infant. An exceptionally fussy patient may do better if fed prior to electrode application; however, feeding usually follows the electrode application.

Electrodes will be applied in accordance with the following procedure to maintain consistency and high-quality recording during the study. Two methods of application are acceptable. The preferred technique is the paste-application method. The head-wrap method with Kling or Coban gauze may also be used.

When evaluating sleep-disordered breathing in the neonate and infant, it is recommended to record both airflow and CO2.

IN MOST CASES, DO NOT PLACE NEONATE OR INFANT IN PRONE POSITION DURING THE POLYSOMNOGRAM. If symptoms occur only in the prone position, this position is to be used with direct observation throughout the evaluation by a caretaker skilled in infant CPR.

Safe sleep guidelines should be followed including but not limited to the use of appropriate sleep environments such as a crib or bassinet, firm mattress surface, and absence of soft bedding or bumpers.

Sleep-study data should be collected for a minimum of 8 hours, if indicated, and should include at least one and preferably two feedings.

Staff must be experienced in the care of infants.

4. Procedure

Prior to initiating procedure:

Confirm physician orders for polysomnogram and any other procedures such as supplemental nocturnal oxygen and/or nasal CPAP.

Confirm that a history and physical are in the patient’s medical chart.

If ordered, confirm proper operation of video camera and recording equipment for audiovisual monitoring.

Contact appropriate nursing staff and arrange mutual time for initiating electrode and sensor application and performing the actual polysomnography when study is to be performed in an inpatient area.

Confirm pediatric resuscitation equipment is in the lab and easily accessible.

Assemble all neonate/infant-related supplies and electrode sensors before patient preparation.

When testing in the NICU area, it may be necessary to change from normal uniform into scrubs and gown specific for that area.

Connect polysomnography equipment and CO2/SpO2 monitor to power source. Locate a power outlet that will not obstruct traffic flow during the sleep study recording. Turn on the computer and monitors. Select appropriate montage and then calibrate SpO2, CO2 and heart-rate signals. After all systems are calibrated and in order, proceed to the electrode and sensor placement/application.

Electrode and sensor placement/application:

WASH HANDS THOROUGHLY

EEG

Preparation of scalp electrodes

The head must be measured according to the international 10-20 system to ensure accurate electrode application. EEG sites generally used for the polysomnogram are Ground, F3, F4, C3, C4, O1, O2, M1, and M2. Additional or substitute electrodes will depend on the montages to be used. A system-reference electrode is also used for computerized systems.

Prepare the electrode sites using a skin abrasive specifically designated for skin preparation. Apply abrasive to marked electrode sites with cotton swab using care to avoid over abrading the scalp. Allow it to dry.

Application of electrode cups can be accomplished by a variety of techniques, either by use of TEN20 conductive paste and/or EC-2 cream, collodion glue or by wrapping patient’s head with Kling or Coban gauze. The paste-application method is preferred.

Place TEN20 conductive paste in electrode cups and apply them to the prepared areas on the scalp. Secure the electrode cups by applying 1" × 1" gauze pieces with a small amount of EC-2 cream over each of the electrode cups and securing them with a few scalp hairs crossed over the gauze patches.

EOG

Properly identify each location of electrode placement and prepare sites with gel to prevent skin abrading and allow to dry.

Fill electrode cups with conductive cream and place the left electrooculogram electrode (EOG) 0.5-1 cm below and 0.5-1 cm lateral to the left outer canthus. Place the right electrooculogram electrode (EOG) 0.5-1 cm above and 0.5-1 cm lateral to the right outer canthus. (Please note that the one cm distance may not be possible on smaller premature infants.)

Tape the electrodes in place. Guide wires toward the back of the patient’s neck.

LEG- EMG

Prep the patient’s legs with alcohol for the EMG electrodes placement. Place the EMG electrodes on the left and right anterior tibialis muscles.

Submental/Mentalis

Place three electrodes to record chin EMG: one in the midline 1 cm above the inferior edge of the mandible, one 1 cm below the inferior edge of the mandible and 1 cm to the right of the midline, one 1 cm below the inferior edge of the mandible and 1 cm to the left of the midline.

EKG

Identify and prepare sites with gel to prevent skin abrasion and allow to dry.

Apply EKG patches and attach snap electrodes. Placement of electrodes should be below the clavicles and equidistant from the sternum.

Respiration:

Airflow devices

A single, double, or triple wire nasal and/or oral thermocouple or thermistor is secured directly below the nostrils to record air-temperature changes at the patient’s nose and/or mouth. A number of different devices are available. The choice of device is at the technologist’s discretion. Nasal pressure transducers should be included whenever possible.

CO2 monitoring (end-tidal or transcutaneous)

A neonate ETCO2 cannula is placed at the patient’s nose to collect end-tidal CO2 information. NOTE: It may be beneficial to tape both sensors together prior to applying to the patient. Care must be taken to avoid occluding the infant’s nostrils with the recording devices.

Respiratory efforts

For monitoring respiratory effort, use one of the following:

esophageal manometry

dual thoracoabdominal RIP belts (calibrated or uncalibrated)

Oximetry

An oximeter probe is attached to patient’s finger, toe, or heel; the choice of site depends on technologist discretion and available sensors.

Final preparation:

Group sensor wires together and secure out of reach of the infant.

Place small socks over both hands to keep infant from pulling wires.

Complete calibration of computer.

Confirm impedances.

Confirm data at polygraph or computer.,

Collection:

Request that NICU nurse or parent begin feeding patient, if appropriate.

Start recording at this time and document “Lights Out.”

Confirm patient’s safety by securing side rails in the up-and-locked position, if applicable.

Document all observed actions and reactions through the use of continuous video. Behavioral patterns and observation are important for infant sleep staging.

Correct any artifact and document it as it occurs during the recording.

Be prepared to include an additional feeding time during collection.

Collection should be at least 8 hours long.

End of sleep study:

End the polysomnogram just prior to “Hands-On” time when the neonate’s nurse is planning to begin patient care.

Document “Lights On.”

End recording and if necessary exit from computer program.

Coordinate direct contact with the neonate’s NICU care nurse.

Gently remove all sensors from the patient’s legs, chest, and face.

Carefully soak each electrode site with warm water until electrode gently lifts away from the patient’s skin. Remove collodion with a product containing only ethanol; never use acetone.

If time allows, ensure that all paste residue has been removed by using a fine-toothed comb through the hair after all electrodes have been removed or a warm, wet washcloth for bald heads.

After the polysomnogram:

Document in patient’s chart that the procedure has been completed and that results are pending according to the facility’s protocol.

Assemble all supplies and equipment and return to the sleep facility.

If computer acquisition systems are used, download the sleep study collection to storage media for scoring purposes as appropriate.

Carefully sort wires and group them together by lengths and application sites.

Remove any remaining tape, wash electrodes with soap and water, rinse and soak in sodium hypochlorite solution for a minimum of 10 minutes. Rinse well and allow it to dry.

Inspect wires at this time to ensure their integrity.

Discard all used disposable items such as naso-oral thermistors, nasal ETCO2 cannulas, nasal oxygen cannulas, and single-patient-use oximeter probes.

Scoring the polysomnogram:

Infants

Sleep scoring for infants up to 2 months of age is based on the current version of the AASM Scoring Manual, as appropriate.

Children

Sleep-stage scoring for children over 2 months of age is based on the most current version of the AASM Scoring Manual, as appropriate.
