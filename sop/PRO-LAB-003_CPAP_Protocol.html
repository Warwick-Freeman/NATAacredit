1. Purpose

To provide the highest quality care for our patients, the sleep lab adheres to the AASM Standards for Accreditation. The accompanying policy and procedure on continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) titrations follow the recommendations of the 2008 Clinical Guidelines for the Manual Titration of Positive Airway Pressure in Patients with Obstructive Sleep Apnea. We recognize that the guidelines are nonbinding, and there may be some minor deviations found within the following policy.

2. Policy

Technologists conducting sleep studies must follow best practices for CPAP titrations to attain optimal pressure setting for their patients. Pressures that are too low may result in suboptimal treatment with continued arousals and awakenings due to residual respiratory events. Determining the appropriate pressure setting for each patient will typically lead to improved adherence and therapeutic outcome. CPAP titration is not an exact science, and the procedure outlined below is meant as a general guideline. Minor variations from these guidelines may be required based on individual patient response.

3. Procedure

Review the patient’s clinical notes for pertinent history.

Review the patient’s previous sleep studies to assess the severity of sleep-disordered breathing, the predominant types of respiratory events, and the position and stage when the events were most severe. This will help the technician anticipate the patients’ pressure needs.

Example: If the patient’s sleep-disordered breathing was worse in the supine position, be sure the patient sleeps in the supine position as much as possible; if the events are primarily REM-related or worse during REM, minimize sleep disruption so that the patient can achieve and maintain REM sleep.

Application of electrodes, montages, filters, sensitivities, and scoring will be performed according to the current version of The AASM Scoring Manual. These guidelines are summarized in the general PSG Protocol.

Prior to the start of the titration study, patients should receive general education on the mechanics of PAP therapy, a hands-on demonstration, careful mask fitting, and a trial of CPAP for acclimatization and desensitization. The length of this process will depend on patient response.

The recommended minimum starting pressure should be 4 cm of water. If the patient is morbidly obese or unable to fall asleep on the setting of 4 cm, higher starting pressures may be needed. Patients returning to the lab for retitration studies may require a higher starting pressure as well.

CPAP should be increased until the following obstructive events are eliminated: apneas, hypopnea, respiratory effort-related arousals (RERAs), and snoring.

CPAP pressures should be raised at least 1 cm if within a 5-minute interval the technician observes any of the following:

Two obstructive apneas

Three obstructive hypopneas

Five RERAs

Ongoing loud snoring (e.g., 3 min of loud or ambiguous snoring).

Pressure setting may need to be increased more quickly during REM sleep given the limited amount of REM during sleep and the need to resolve events during this stage.

If the patient awakens and complains that the CPAP pressure is too high, return to a lower pressure that the patient reports as comfortable enough to allow a return to sleep.

Exploratory CPAP pressures above the pressure required to control all of the above respiratory events are permissible if approved by the director, but should not exceed 5 cm.

PAP mask refit or readjustment should be performed whenever any significant unintentional leak is observed. Mask leaks should be corrected before raising CPAP pressure. Once the mask leak has been fixed, decrease the pressure to the last setting where mouth breathing and/or mask leakage was not present, and then re-titrate as indicated. Make sure to document directly on the study the steps taken to resolve the leak and the type of masks used.

The recommended maximum CPAP pressure is 20 cm for adult patients. If the patient is uncomfortable or intolerant of high CPAP pressures, consideration should be given to a switch to bilevel positive airway pressure (BiPAP). BiPAP may also be initiated if there are continued respiratory events at a CPAP pressure of 15 cm.

BiPAP may also be indicated if the patient expresses difficulty exhaling against a high CPAP pressure or develops frequent central apneas in response to higher CPAP pressures. Document directly on the study of why the patient is being switched from CPAP to BiPAP.

The recoding technologist should document directly on the study at least every 30 minutes. Documentation should include sleep stage, sleep position, current PAP pressure and status of respiratory events or control.

If the patient develops central apneas, pressure setting may need to be lowered.

Ideally, the patient should be recorded in supine REM sleep for at least 15 minutes at the presumed optimal pressure during the PAP titration study. If the patient is in REM sleep but not in the supine position while at the presumed optimal pressure, the technician should gently awaken the patient and instruct them to lie in the supine position.

The final CPAP pressure selected for patient use following the titration study should reflect adequate control of the patient’s respiratory events, (preferably an RDI <5 per hour) a minimum SpO2 above 90%, and a leak within acceptable parameters at the pressure.

If the patient is unable to tolerate CPAP due to 1) Persistent mouth breathing despite use of a full-face mask/chin strap; 2) Inability to exhale against higher expiratory pressures (typically beginning anywhere from 15 to 20 cm of water); or 3) Has frequent central apneas; the use of bilevel positive airway pressure may be indicated. Document directly in the study the rationale for switching the patient from CPAP to BiPAP.

Ensure that supine sleep has been seen on the chosen setting. Going above the chosen setting by one or two cm of water to show range may be helpful to ensure that the correct pressure has been established.
